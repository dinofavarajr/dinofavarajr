## The Ultimate Short Form Video Structure

Did You Think I Forgot About You?

I would never do that!

The past 30 days have been wild for me. In the last month, I've received over 5 Million views on my content... ISN'T THAT CRAZY?

How was I able to make these videos that popped off?

I've easily posted over 1000 videos on TikTok. I've analyzed the retention on the last 200.

You might be asking: "How do I make videos that get more than 200 views?"

Here's my process:

Noob Mindset: "Just post. Quantity over Quantity"
Pro Mindset: "Quantity teaches you Quality"

Packaging your video is often the variable of success... Not how many videos you make.

In an episode with Joe Rogan, Mr. Beast says "You can 10x your viewership if you have a better idea." But that's only half the battle...

Here's EXACTLY how to structure a TikTok video to maximize results:

1. Title (First second of video)
2. Hook (first 3-5 seconds)
3. Caption (the "bait")
4. "Sub-hook" (the promise of the video)
5. Body
6. Call to Action

**Title** — The very first thing on screen. Think of it like the "other piece of bread" on a burger — the caption is the complementary piece. Make it 3-5 words. Focus on urgency and curiosity.

**Hook** — Copy what works. Find what other creators have done and adapt it to your content. You want your hook to inspire curiosity and provide tension to the viewer. You want them to feel as if they are about to miss out on a HUGE opportunity if they don't watch the next 10 seconds.

**Caption** — Imagine that this is the EXTRA hook that people see right after the main hook. Overload the viewer with "I have to watch this." A good caption should include some humor that only makes sense if they watched the end of the video.

**Body** — Keep clips short and snappy. Change your background or location every 2-3 seconds. Keep sentences short. Stop recording after each sentence and record a new clip.

**Call to Action** — In under 3 seconds, tell them what to do. BUT ONLY PICK ONE. Don't ask them to "Like AND Follow." Either "Follow for more" or "Comment what I should discuss next."

So What Now?

We now are almost FULLY equipped to start CRUSHING it on TikTok. We've grasped what makes something viral, different formats, and how to structure a notification-stopping video.

I'm About To Step On Some Toes...

There's a TON of advice out there. And I'm gonna be honest... Most of it is tra$h.

Here's the #1 thing that determines if your video is going to perform well... Is Your Video Good?

Here's the top 3 pieces of terrible advice, why they're wrong, and what to do instead:

**"Post 3-5x a Day"** — There's a reason people tell you this. It's because it works... for SOME people. But for most, it doesn't. Quantity helps you find out what works — that's the only reason to do it. Don't post for the sake of posting.

**"Use Trending Hashtags to Get Views"** — This is not true. You will not "get views" simply because a magical hashtag will show your video to 100,000 people.

My Advice: Use hashtags, but with purpose:
1. To DIFFERENTIATE your content and group it together (so TikTok shows more of your videos to fans)
2. To keep track of views — create unique hashtags per topic to easily see how many views each gets

**"Post at Peak Times of Day"** — This is the biiiiiiggest lie. It literally does NOT matter. Post when you want — before you go to bed, after work, whenever. Post and forget about it. It's emotionally, mentally, and psychologically taxing to constantly think about your videos. Get on TikTok, create a video you are proud of, and then get off.

**Is Your Video Good?** — Here's how to figure it out: How many views did it get? That's it. If a video is good, it will get views. No fancy tricks or special posting schedule will fix a bad video.

So if you want to make GOOD videos, you need to learn how to test ideas and analyze data. I'll show you EXACTLY how I do it next week.

See you then. -Dino

How to CONSISTENTLY Turn a Boring Video into a Compelling Storyline and Convince Anyone to Watch Till the End

So most likely you have read the last 6 of my emails. And we're at the last step in the process: How to get people to watch till the end.

Well I have some good news: It's simpler than you think.

Here's the bad news: It's more difficult than you think.

- Confess a problem
- Accuse someone/something

## this one sentence keeps a viewer from swiping

| | |
|---|---|
| **Newsletter** | Short Form Secrets (Kit.com) |

| **Tags** | Educational, Actionable, Viral Growth, YouTube Shorts, TikTok |

You Can Make ANY Video Exciting with this SIMPLE Idea...

You know what I love? Improv.

A year ago, I took two improv classes locally here in Orlando, FL from SAK Comedy Labs. I used to think I was amazing at improv (#theaterkid)... until I took the intro class.

I did a scene with three other players who I thought were absolute garbage. I was the main character of the scene and it seemed to not be going anywhere. So I did what I thought I was supposed to do: took over. I started maniacally screaming, forcing people into positions, and trying to salvage the scene.

The teacher yelled "SCENE" and then looked me right in the eyes: "You just steamrolled the scene. You pushed everyone out of the way trying to make it interesting and instead, you made it even worse."

Outside of humbling myself greatly, I learned something EXTREMELY powerful about the art of improvisation: "Yes... AND?"

The concept: In improv, you are NEVER supposed to say "no." You always say "Yes" and then ADD to the situation. You build on what others have said.

The reason this makes scenes SO MUCH MORE ENTERTAINING is because it creates tension and uncertainty.

This is exactly what good video storytelling does. Each piece of the video says "Yes... AND?"

- "Yes, I learned how to go viral... AND I found a channel that was doing it secretly"
- "Yes, I found the channel... AND it turned out to be my hero"
- "Yes, it's my hero... AND he's been doing it for years without telling anyone"

You never know where the story is going next. That's the "Yes... AND?" of storytelling.

Example (like a Michael Scott improv scene):

BOOM!!! Officer Michael Faccario here! GET ON THE GROUND!!!
Michael? From HIGH SCHOOL!?

Next time you're filming, make sure to mid-way through the video:
- Confess something
- Accuse someone
- Discover something

PRO TIP: The line I use ALL the time: "I was getting nervous it wouldn't workout." It helps showcase to the viewer that it might not be all sunshine and daisies.

buh bye ✌🏼 -Dino

"I barely get to do what I love anymore because I'm stuck doing all the menial tasks that come with running a creative business… it's hard."

He was very quick to shut me down.

"Farming is hard. But thank God I am not having to rely on my farming acumen to survive. We get the opportunity to do creative work for a living. If you look at it through a grateful lens, it will become fun again."

You ever listen to white noise? It's a very distinct and loud sound that you KNOW is there, but since it's continuous, you start to zone out and forget about it. Only when you remember the white noise is ON do you remember to hear it.

That's how this one felt. I smacked my forehead and let out an internal "duh." American culture loves to highlight Grit and Hard Work. Push through the hard times because the good stuff is on the other side!

While there is some truth to that, I'm reminded that pushing through hardship isn't solved by "toughing it out" but by leaning into gratitude.

Belief > Love

I shared with Julian that I fell in love with creating YouTube videos at a very early age. A passion that I've held onto for a very long time.

He told me: "Don't tell me you love it. Tell me you believe in it."

Instantly I knew this was the final draft of the previous statement. Love is subjective. There's plenty of pieces of work I've made in the past that I love. But usually what I LOVE is the final product, not the work itself to get there. Belief, not love, is what gives you the energy for longevity.

Last fall I KNEW I wanted to create a weekly vlog documenting Jenny and I's pregnancy leading up to the birth of our first son. I would set them to release daily as soon as our son was born. It was a digital time capsule for Jenny and I to enjoy together. It had to exist.

This would add about 10-15 hours a week editing each video at night when Jenny would go to bed. Late nights and early mornings editing a video while trying to capture footage throughout the week was rough. Always thinking of the next vid while trying to finish up the previous one. It was hard, and at some points was definitely not "fun"

But I believed in the final product. And it got me through to the end.

Never Look at Comments

The Weirdest Piece of Advice was probably when I mentioned dealing with internet hate. Julian just cut me off and told me to "never look at comments."

He was INSISTENT on this. I'm not sure if I necessarily agree, but from an artist's perspective I can see his point. Art is not something to look for feedback on. Art comes from within and the second you start letting external opinions get in the way, you will lose.

After hearing this, I definitely wish I had heeded a bit more of this advice last year when the entire internet ganged up on me about Mr. Beast. Feel free to read more about that here:

May 10, 2024

"You Always Make It About Yourself"

I read this sentence at fourteen years old and it destroyed me.

I'm sitting next to my childhood best friend, David, and he nudges my arm. I look over and he motions with his eyes for me to read the sentences on his flip phone. We're in the middle of a glacier tour in Seward, Alaska for his 15th birthday trip.

He got to pick one friend to go with him. I was stoked.

We're about halfway through the trip and we're having a blast. His grandparents, Ti and Dex, have been taking care of us during this entire expedition and doing everything they can to make it one of the most memorable trips we may ever go on. They do this really cool thing with their grandkids where they would take them anywhere in the world for their 15th birthday. David chose Alaska.

On this particular moment, we are about a week into our journey; which is enough time for even the closest of friends to start noticing annoying traits and frustrating habits. We were sitting side by side on a ferry directed towards a giant glacier (for tourism purposes). It was 2008 so we both were rocking some sick flip phones. Although we didn't have any service in this specific location, we still had the text box to use as our digital notepad.

After a few minutes of sitting down in silence, David nudges me and shows me his screen. Written out via T9 text is a paragraph of expressed frustration about how I've been acting over the last week:

"You are acting extremely selfish. This is my birthday trip. I asked you to come along as a guest. You're my best friend but over the last seven days you have been-"

He runs out of room so he brings back his phone to delete everything and type out a new phrase:

"You always make it about yourself"

Was this a joke? Is he trying to pull a bit? I look up into his eyes. He stares at me with a gaze that blatantly states: "Did I stutter?"

I take a few seconds, stand up, and excuse myself to the restroom. I take a walk towards the downstairs of the ferry and find a corner by myself to stare out the window.

I cry.

My first thought was one of pure betrayal. How could he say that? Does my best friend hate me? Is he not my friend anymore?

My next mental step is realizing that he was 100% correct. Ever look back on memories and truly realize how your perspective was all wrong? Without even realizing it or being conscious of it, I was portraying narcissistic tendencies and behaviors. As soon as David mentioned it, all these memories flooded back to me from a new perspective.

This moment taught me a lesson that would go on to be the most valuable skill I hold dear: Self-Awareness.

From then on I learned to think about the bigger picture. Was I being too loud? Was I talking about myself too much? When's the last time I asked a question to another person? Ricky has been quiet a while, maybe we should bring him into the conversation. Did I say something rude? I should address it as soon as I can.

Immature people steal attention from others.
Mature Leaders redistribute attention evenly.

Most people who have a knack for performance and entertainment tend to remain immature in their self awareness. They act and think like I did as an eighth grader. They try to get the laugh and refocus attention on themselves. I look around the circle of people who are being tricked into focusing on just one individual, and I decide it's not fair. So I steal the attention back myself. I push back against the other individual controlling the circle and force myself into the spotlight for one purpose only: to shine it on someone else.

This often looks like telling a story about myself only to look someone else in the eyes and ask them if they have a similar story. I crack a joke at my own expense only to give credit to someone else who's been quiet for most of the conversation. All the while, trying to fight the urge to bask in the spotlight myself for too long.

This is something I want all my children to know. I believe the world would be a better place if more people were self aware of how they make others feel.

Start doing this not just with the words you say, but the actions you take. Think through and view your actions from the lens of the other person. Not just how YOU would react to this action, but how do you think THEY would react.

Self-Awareness is a social super power that can benefit you in ANY aspect of your life. In many vocations, Self-Awareness is MANDATORY and without it you will fail.

Sometimes when I tell this story, some people may feel this comes across as harsh. "Why would he say that!?" is the typical response.

But I believe he did for me what others weren't willing to do: reveal a blindspot. He shone a light on a weakness I had which I couldn't see on my own. This is something a GREAT Friend does for you. They're willing to call you out on your BS and hold you accountable for things you might not catch.

H.A.G.S.

Dino, Jr.

On Becoming a Spiritual Man

I recently asked my beautiful wife, Jenny, a question:

"What are you interested in me writing about?"

She told me to write about "becoming a man of faith."

To be fair, I think I'm mediocre at best. I don't really think I'm anything special. Yet, I can still humbly say I know who I am.

DISCLAIMER: This is in no way a replacement for reading the Bible. The Bible contains everything you might need to grow in your faith. Podcasts, books, newsletters, and worship music are all optional and helpful tools to growth.

My Three Favorite Biblical Characters

"Where do I even start?"

Moses — The Reluctant Hero

Moses had every reason NOT to lead the Israelites out of Egypt. He had a speech impediment, he was raised by the enemy, and he was 80 years old when God called on him. Yet he still went. His story reminds me that God doesn't call the qualified — He qualifies the called.

Takeaway: "Be strong and courageous. Do not be afraid or terrified because of them, for the LORD your God goes with you; he will never leave you nor forsake you." — Deuteronomy 31:6. Something that has constantly attacked me is discouragement. This verse reminds me that the Lord goes BEFORE me and conquers.

Jesus — The Greatest Leader

Absolute menace to society in the best way. How often do we get asked hard questions, only to think we have to have all the answers? Jesus was living proof that we are not here to answer every question in the universe. We are here to show love to those who need it and spread the Gospel.

Jesus was counter-culture, outspoken, kind, slow, and inquisitive. He sat down at tables and shared meals. He invested in those who were hungry for affection. He looked AT people instead of through them. He made eye contact and listened to their words.

Attributes of Jesus all men should strive to emulate:
- LOVE Your Mom — Call your mom. Talk to your mom. Listen to your mom.
- Be Countercultural — Go against the grain of culture when it conflicts with your values.
- Ask More Questions Than You Answer — You don't need to have all the answers.
- Invest in People Who Are Hungry — Don't waste your time on people who don't want it.

King Saul — The Cautionary Tale

Saul had every advantage: tall, handsome, chosen by God. Yet he constantly worried about how others perceived him, let emotions drive his actions, and always tried to prove he was great instead of simply striving to be great.

When he was announced as KING by Samuel, instead of confidently stepping forward, he chose to hide in a barrel.

King David — A Man After God's Own Heart

David started from the bottom — a shepherd, youngest of twelve brothers. He spent time with the Lord and dwelled in Him. Yet even he made mistakes. Habits from David to avoid: sitting too long in comfort, and relishing in your own works.

Becoming a Man of God is something I'm still striving after. Something I can't fully become but I can always aim to get closer to. Our walk with the Lord is a continual work towards becoming more righteous.

I feel very, very lucky in my life and I believe a lot of the blessings I experience are because I've had the Lord with me.

H.A.G.S. ✌🏼

🦕 Dino
